## The General Assembly: Democratic Stewardship

The **General Assembly (GA)** is the supreme decision-making authority of the Lean C.R.O.W.D. It represents the voices of employees, client-members, and fund-contributors in a truly democratic process.

### Membership

The GA includes representatives from all three pillars:

**Employees** — All employees with at least 3 months tenure have full voting rights.

**Client-Members** — Clients who actively participate in the Community of Practice and share a long-term commitment to the mission have voting rights proportional to their engagement (typically 1 vote per member organization).

**Fund-Contributors** — Individuals and organizations providing significant financial support have voting representation.

### Key Responsibilities

The General Assembly holds ultimate authority over:

| Decision                   | Details                                              |
| -------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| **Amend the Manifesto**    | Changes to core mission and regenerative principles  |
| **Elect the Board**        | Selection of 3-5 board members for 2-year terms      |
| **Approve Annual Reports** | Review financial, impact, and organizational reports |
| **Strategic Direction**    | Approve the 3-5 year vision and major initiatives    |
| **Budget Allocation**      | Approve annual budget and major financial decisions  |
| **Major Pivots**           | Approve new service lines or fundamental changes     |
| **Conflict Resolution**    | Address disputes between stakeholders                |
| **Governance Changes**     | Modify bylaws and governance structures              |

### Meeting Schedule

**Full Assembly** — Quarterly meetings where all members can vote on major decisions.

| Meeting | Focus                                             |
| ------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| **Q1**  | Annual review, strategic planning, Board election |
| **Q2**  | Mid-year assessment, adjustment to strategy       |
| **Q3**  | Research findings, methodology updates            |
| **Q4**  | Annual impact review, planning next year          |

**Steering Committee** — Monthly meetings with elected representatives to handle routine governance and prepare for full assemblies.

**Special Sessions** — Called ad hoc for urgent matters requiring immediate decision-making.

### How Decisions Are Made

**Standard Process:**

1. **Proposal** — A stakeholder or the Board proposes a decision
2. **Discussion** — GA members discuss the proposal, raise concerns
3. **Deliberation** — The proposal is refined based on feedback
4. **Vote** — Simple majority (50%+1) passes the decision

**Consensus Process** (for major changes):

- Used for manifesto amendments, Board elections, major pivots
- Requires 70% affirmative vote, or proposal is revised and retried
- Designed to ensure broad support, not just technical majority

**Vetoes** (Conflict Check):

- If any stakeholder group (employees, members, contributors) believes a decision fundamentally contradicts the mission, they can request a conflict review
- The decision is revisited; the vetoing group must articulate the ethical concern
- If valid, the decision is rejected or revised

### Transparency & Documentation

**Every decision is documented** including:

- The proposal and its rationale
- All amendments and changes
- The vote count
- Dissenting views (if requested)
- The implementation plan

This documentation is published to the community (with appropriate confidentiality protections).

### Conflicts & Edge Cases

**What if the Board disagrees with the GA?**
The GA has ultimate authority. The Board serves the GA, not the reverse. If a conflict is irreconcilable, Board members can resign or be replaced.

**What if employees and members have conflicting interests?**
This is where the Triad model strengthens us. The third pillar (fund-contributors) and the Board often play a mediating role, seeking solutions that serve the mission overall.

**What if a decision is made that undermines the mission?**
This is rare because all stakeholders are filtered by mission alignment. But if it happens, the next GA session can reverse the decision (75% majority required).

### Participation & Accessibility

We make participation feasible:

- **Multiple time zones** — Meetings are recorded; asynchronous voting is available
- **Diverse participation** — We provide translation, accessibility tools, childcare support
- **Clear information** — Documentation is written in plain language, not jargon-heavy
- **Good faith assumption** — We assume everyone is genuinely trying to serve the mission
- **Learning support** — New members receive orientation on how GA works

### Examples of GA Decisions

**Strategic Example:**
"Should we expand the fellowship program from 3 to 5 fellows this year, and allocate an additional software engineer to mentorship?"

**Decision Process:**

- HR proposes based on research showing demand
- Members discuss impact on quality of mentorship
- Finance projects budget implications
- Employees express excitement about new fellows
- Vote: Approved 85% affirmative
- Implementation: Expand pipeline, hire additional mentor

**Conflict Example:**
"Should we take a project for a military contractor?"

**Decision Process:**

- A potential client offers lucrative contract
- Proposal goes to GA for consideration
- Employees and members debate ethical implications
- Think-tank research is reviewed (does software enhance or restrict autonomy?)
- Fund-contributors note impact on mission credibility
- A conflict veto is raised: "This violates our DORITH principle"
- GA votes: Reject the project 92% against
- Alternative: C-level explores how to serve the client's legitimate needs differently

---

**The General Assembly is how we ensure that power isn't concentrated in any single leader or group.** Instead, we rely on the collective intelligence and ethical commitment of our communities.

It's harder than top-down decision-making. It's also more robust, more creative, and more aligned with our regenerative values.
