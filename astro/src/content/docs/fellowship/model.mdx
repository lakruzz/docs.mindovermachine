## The Regenerative Fellowship Model

The traditional internship model treats junior developers as cheap labor. They get coffee, grunt work, and hopefully some learning along the way.

**We reject that model.**

Our Fellowship is based on **Legitimate Peripheral Participation (LPP)** — the idea that learning happens through engagement in a real community of practice, not in isolation.

### The Problem With Traditional Internships

**Standard internship:**

1. Interns do "safe" tasks that don't interrupt real work
2. Knowledge transfer is minimal (interns figure it out)
3. Interns leave with portfolio pieces but not deep understanding
4. Organization gets cheap labor, not future leaders

**Result:** Interns are treated as temporary, disposable.

### Our Alternative: Legitimate Peripheral Participation

**Interns are legitimate members** of the community, participating authentically in real work.

**"Peripheral" doesn't mean excluded.** It means learning through observation, scaffolded support, and gradually increasing responsibility.

**The progression:**

1. **Observation** — Attend design meetings, code reviews, planning sessions
2. **Apprenticeship** — Work alongside experienced practitioners
3. **Scaffolded work** — Given manageable tasks with support
4. **Growing autonomy** — Gradually moving to independent work
5. **Mentorship of others** — By end of year, helping new fellows

### The Five Pillars of Learning

Fellows master the **C.R.O.W.D.** curriculum:

#### C — Curious (Comprehensive Thinking)

Learning to "coordinate everything comprehensively" using Buckminster Fuller's engineering philosophy.

**Activities:**

- Participate in systems thinking workshops
- Practice mapping problem domains holistically
- Learn to ask "how does this ripple through the system?"

#### R — Regenerative Mastery

Identifying digital waste and assisting in code optimization.

**Activities:**

- Code audits for energy efficiency
- Lifecycle analysis exercises
- Contributing to green algorithm improvements

#### O — Operational Excellence

Foundational training on The Toyota Way and lean principles.

**Activities:**

- Learn the 14 principles of Toyota Way
- Understand Kanban and one-piece flow
- Practice identifying and eliminating waste

#### W — Whole System Stewardship

Participating in board-approved open-source projects.

**Activities:**

- Make contributions to FOSS projects
- Attend OS community events
- Publish about contributing to cultural heritage

#### D — DORITH (Do the Right Thing)

Practicing reflective journaling and ethical decision-making.

**Activities:**

- Maintain a reflective practice journal
- Participate in ethics discussions
- Practice communicating with integrity

### The Learning Journey

**Month 1–2: Observation & Onboarding**

- Attend all team meetings and community sessions
- Observe experienced engineers working
- Read documentation and code
- Complete foundational learning modules
- Outcome: Understanding of the organization and principles

**Month 3–4: Scaffolded Contribution**

- Take on small, manageable tasks (bugs, documentation)
- Get detailed feedback from mentors
- Pair with senior engineers on medium tasks
- Start participating in design discussions
- Outcome: First meaningful contributions, growing confidence

**Month 5–7: Growing Authority**

- Lead small features (with oversight)
- Conduct code reviews of peer work
- Take on more complex components
- Mentor new fellows (if you're past Month 6)
- Outcome: Independent professional capability

**Month 8–9: Visible Impact**

- Own significant project components
- Lead design decisions (with peer review)
- Contribute meaningfully to open source
- Participate in governance discussions
- Outcome: Seen as valuable team member

**Month 10–12: Leadership & Transition**

- Mentor incoming fellows
- Propose new initiatives
- Represent team at community engagement
- Plan for what's next (permanent role, other opportunity, etc.)
- Outcome: Ready for the next phase of career

### The Role of Mentorship

**Every fellow has a mentor** — an experienced engineer (typically L4–L5) who:

- **Provides guidance** — "Here's how we approach this type of problem"
- **Gives feedback** — Direct, kind, specific input
- **Protects learning** — Ensures the fellow gets meaningful work
- **Advocates** — Makes sure the fellow is seen and valued
- **Plans growth** — Helps the fellow develop their path forward

**Mentorship is 5–10% of the mentor's time** — built into their job, not extra.

### Advancement to Associate (L2)

**After 12 months, you'll be evaluated against the L1 Capability Matrix criteria:**

- Have you demonstrated green algorithm understanding?
- Can you independently execute regenerative SDLC tasks?
- Are you contributing meaningfully to open source?
- Do you embody DORITH in decisions?

**If yes → You advance to Associate and the standard career path.**

**If not yet → Options:** Renewal for another fellowship year, extension in current role, or transition to other opportunities.

### Why This Works

**For fellows:**

- Real experience, not busywork
- Genuine mentorship and guidance
- Path to meaningful career
- Salary from day 1
- Part of something larger than yourself

**For the organization:**

- Developing future leaders
- Infusing regenerative principles from the start
- Building healthy culture (not extractive)
- Long-term talent pipeline
- Contributing to profession development

**For the profession:**

- Creating next generation of conscious engineers
- Modeling how internships should work
- Advancing regenerative principles in the industry
- Demonstrating that ethics and business viability coexist
