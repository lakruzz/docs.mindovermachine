## The Triad Model: Democratic Stewardship

The Lean C.R.O.W.D. is governed by a unique model that creates alignment between three essential stakeholders. We call it **The Triad**.

### The Three Pillars

```txt
             General Assembly
            /      |      \
    Employees   Clients    Contributors
    (People)   (Members)   (Resources)
```

Every significant governance decision involves all three:

#### 1. **Employees — The Executors**

Employees are the engineers, researchers, and facilitators who execute the mission.

**Their role in governance:**

- Vote on manifesto changes and strategic direction
- Elect the Board of Directors
- Participate in the General Assembly meetings
- Hold each other accountable to regenerative principles

**Their stake:**

- Meaningful work aligned with their values
- Transparent compensation and clear advancement paths
- Democratic voice in organizational decisions
- Shared benefits through profit-sharing and bonuses

#### 2. **Clients (Members) — The Partners**

Unlike traditional consultancy hierarchies, our clients become **members** of the Community of Practice.

**What does membership mean?**

- Active participation in the General Assembly
- Shared decision-making about strategic direction
- Engagement in long-term transformations, not one-off projects
- Contribution to joint efforts and research
- Access to our talent pool and network

**Their stake:**

- Software that aligns with their values
- Participatory design ensuring their needs are centered
- Long-term partnership, not vendor-customer relationship
- Influence over which Open Source projects we fund
- Membership in a global community of regenerative practitioners

#### 3. **Fund-Contributors — The Supporters**

Fund-contributors provide financial support for the mission.

This could include:

- Impact investors committed to regenerative outcomes
- Foundations funding social-technical innovation
- Individuals who believe in the mission
- Institutional partners

**Their role in governance:**

- Vote on strategic financial decisions
- Receive reports on impact and use of funds
- Participate in General Assembly if they choose
- Influence how non-profit funds are allocated

**Their stake:**

- Clear accountability for how funds are used
- Demonstrated impact through rigorous measurement
- Alignment with the mission and values
- Membership in a community driving systemic change

### The General Assembly: Supreme Authority

The General Assembly is the sovereign decision-making body. It includes representatives from all three pillars.

**Key responsibilities:**

| Responsibility             | Details                                      |
| -------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| **Amend the Manifesto**    | Changes to core values and principles        |
| **Elect the Board**        | Selection of governance oversight            |
| **Approve Annual Reports** | Review of impact, financials, and progress   |
| **Strategic Direction**    | 3-5 year vision and major initiatives        |
| **Major Decisions**        | Budget allocation, new service lines, pivots |
| **Conflict Resolution**    | Addressing tensions between stakeholders     |

**Meeting Structure:**

- **Quarterly** full assemblies (all members)
- **Monthly** steering committees (elected representatives)
- **ad hoc** special meetings for urgent matters

### The Professional Board: Strategic Guidance

The Professional Board is elected by the General Assembly and serves as an advisory body.

**Key responsibilities:**

| Responsibility           | Details                                             |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------- |
| **Fiduciary Oversight**  | Financial management and non-profit compliance      |
| **Strategic Planning**   | Co-creating 3-5 year vision with C-level            |
| **Ethical Guidance**     | Advising on integrity and mission alignment         |
| **Open Source Approval** | Reviewing and approving OS project allocations      |
| **Risk Management**      | Identifying and mitigating organizational risks     |
| **Stakeholder Advocacy** | Representing community interests and emerging needs |

The Board typically includes:

- 1-2 employee representatives
- 1-2 client-member representatives
- 1-2 external advisors with relevant expertise
- Optional fund-contributor representative

### C-Level Leaders (CxO): Mission Execution

C-level leaders (Chief Executive Officer and similar roles) are responsible for day-to-day execution of the strategy approved by the General Assembly.

**Key responsibilities:**

| Responsibility             | Details                                                |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Operational Excellence** | Managing the Regenerative SDLC and consulting delivery |
| **Team Leadership**        | Hiring, development, and performance of employees      |
| **Client Relations**       | Orchestrating member relationships and shared efforts  |
| **Innovation**             | Researching and testing new approaches                 |
| **Representation**         | Speaking for the organization to external stakeholders |
| **Accountability**         | Reporting regularly to the Board and GA                |

C-level leaders are appointed by the Board, not elected. They serve at the pleasure of the Board and can be removed if they lose alignment with the mission.

### Employees: Mechanical Application

Employees execute the regenerative principles across all projects and operations.

**Key responsibilities:**

| Responsibility               | Details                                                |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Regenerative Engineering** | Building software according to regenerative principles |
| **Participatory Design**     | Engaging end-users holistically in design              |
| **Open Source Contribution** | Dedicating time to the global commons                  |
| **Reflective Practice**      | Continuous learning and ethical self-assessment        |
| **Knowledge Sharing**        | Publishing insights and field guides                   |
| **Mentorship**               | Developing junior teammates                            |

### Avoiding Conflicts of Interest

With three stakeholder groups, conflicts can arise. We prevent them through:

**Clear Separation:**

- Members (clients) cannot be employees
- Employees cannot have personal business interests conflicting with the mission
- Board members recuse themselves from decisions where they have conflicts

**Transparency:**

- All major decisions are documented and explained
- Salary levels and advancement criteria are public
- Open Source funding decisions are transparent
- Financial statements are shared openly

**Democratic Accountability:**

- Any stakeholder can raise concerns with the GA
- Conflicts are mediated by neutral board members
- If unresolved, the full GA votes on solutions

### The Balance

This model creates dynamic tension in a healthy way:

- **Employees** push for meaningful work and fair treatment
- **Client-Members** push for quality outcomes and long-term value
- **Fund-Contributors** push for financial sustainability and measurable impact
- **The Board** ensures all voices are heard and aligned

This tension keeps the organization honest and mission-focused.

---

**The Triad Model is our answer to the question:** How do you build an organization that serves multiple stakeholders without compromising integrity?

Answer: You give everyone a seat at the table, align incentives around impact, and trust the process of democratic stewardship.
